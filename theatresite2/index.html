A brief glance at the history of the monologue.

Looking at different monologues throughout time: in Ancient Greece and Rome, Shakespearean Soliloquies in in the Elizabethan Era, and the works of Franca Rame in the late 20th century

Who doesn't love the sound of their own voice? Definitely not playwrights, that's for sure. The monologue is one of the most iconic and powerful conventions of theatre. Throughout this project, I've explored different types of monologues and their origins, starting with theatre in Ancient Greece and Rome, then taking a look at Shakespeare's soliloquies, before ending with the works of Franca Rame. To Conclude

Now you've reached the end. You've sat through videos of me monologuing about monologues, listened to my auditory analyses. Or you haven't clicked through to the other pages and just scrolled down straight away- in which case, I strongly suggest you go take a look at those other pages. Please. I put a lot of effort into this. At least go and wave your mouse over the drawings on the pages to watch them move. That took me way longer than it really should have.

The monologue is a powerful tool, in the right hands. It is a way of connecting directly with the audience, and drawing them in to the play. Shakespeare used this as a way to reveal the inner thoughts of his characters to the audience, giving the audience secrets that they wouldn't otherwise know. Franca Rame wrote whole stage monologues, used to confront the audience and force them to witness and take part in the suffering being shown. It's an inherently intimate feature of theatre, and that is what I found so appealing about it. Those classical playrights and Shakespeare and Rame all used monologues in different ways to each other, and yet the core of it- that action of reaching out to the audience and establishing a connection- remains the same.
